### VP-P0-008 — Canada — scholarship planning membership for high school students

- Specialist phase: Usable
- Specialist ceiling/runtime observation: Not instrumented in this run
- Bob synthesis runtime: Not instrumented in this run
- Pass/fail: Pass with intervention required
- Reason: The idea became commercially usable only after narrowing to a parent-paid scholarship execution membership that turns deadlines, essay drafts, and eligibility tracking into one disciplined application workflow rather than generic student planning support.
- Commercial notes:
  - The strongest version was not broad academic coaching; it was a scholarship operations membership for families targeting high-cost post-secondary pathways.
  - Best twist was an essay-asset and deadline orchestration layer that reuses application material across multiple scholarships instead of treating each application as a fresh scramble.
  - Market logic was credible because motivated families already spend on tutoring and admissions help, but buyer-user split and seasonality make retention less clean than classic membership categories.
  - Monetisation worked best as seasonal cohorts or annual family plans with premium review support, but genericity risk returns fast if the workflow edge is not kept explicit.

- Specialist ceiling/runtime observation: Not instrumented in this run
- Bob synthesis runtime: Not instrumented in this run

## Batch 3 summary

- Cases completed: 6
- Passes: 2
- Fails: 2
- Conditional passes: 2
- Notable recurring failure modes:
  - Generic concierge, community, or support framing that never hardens into a proprietary workflow or asset.
  - Ideas that start with real emotional pain but stay undecided between software, service, membership, and marketplace forms.
  - Consumer concepts that only become viable after very aggressive narrowing around a repeatable operational job.
  - Trust-heavy family-support categories where vague language immediately undermines willingness to pay.
- Production-readiness effect:
  - Batch 3 improved confidence modestly for operational workflow and service-layer businesses where the system can find a concrete recurring job and a defensible twist.
  - Batch 3 did not improve confidence on broad consumer concierge/support inputs; those still fail unless the system forces a much harder business definition.
  - Net read: confidence improved slightly versus earlier batches, but the hot path is still not production-safe across vague consumer inputs.
